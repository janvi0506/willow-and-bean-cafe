About The Willow And Bean Cafe

The Willow And Bean Cafe is more than just a coffee shop ☕ — it's a cozy neighborhood haven where conversations brew alongside freshly ground beans. Founded in with a vision to blend comfort with craft, our café was born out of a deep love for coffee, community, and calm corners. Nestled in the heart of the city, The Willow And Bean offers a carefully curated menu of classic brews, artisanal bites, and indulgent desserts, all served in a space designed to feel like a warm hug. Whether you're meeting a friend, working on your next big idea, or simply taking a moment for yourself, our café invites you to slow down and savor life — one cup at a time. We believe in sustainable sourcing, local partnerships, and fostering a space where creativity and connection thrive over coffee.

Our Story

The Willow And Bean Cafe began with a tiny idea scribbled in a well-loved notebook during a rainy monsoon afternoon in Mumbai. Our founder, a lifelong coffee enthusiast, wanted to create a café that felt like a warm corner of a bookshop—where every cup has a backstory, and every guest becomes part of the narrative. What started as a dream soon became a local favorite, where regulars know each other by name and where the aroma of freshly ground beans signals the start of something comforting.

Our Mission

At The Willow And Bean, our mission is to create an inclusive space where everyone—from first-time coffee sippers to lifelong aficionados—feels welcome. We strive to serve not just great coffee, but meaningful moments, while supporting ethical sourcing, mindful eating and community values.

Why "The Willow And Bean"?

The name symbolizes everything we love: the willow tree represents calm, shade, and comfort—just like our cozy seating corners. The bean, of course, is a nod to our love for coffee. Together, they embody our café's spirit: peaceful, rooted, and full of warmth.

Our Values

  • Local First: We work with local bakers and roasters to bring you fresh, flavorful offerings daily.
  • Eco-Friendly: From compostable cups to zero-waste kitchen practices, we're serious about sustainability.
  • Community-Driven: We host monthly open mics, book clubs, and artist showcases to support local talent.

Coffee Trivia

When did coffee first appear in India?

Coffee was first introduced to India in the 17th century, around 1670, by a Sufi saint named Baba Budan. During his pilgrimage to Mecca, Baba Budan traveled through Yemen, where he encountered coffee and became fascinated by it. At the time, the Arab world tightly controlled coffee cultivation, allowing only roasted beans to be exported. Defying this restriction, Baba Budan smuggled seven raw coffee beans back to India by hiding them in his robe. Upon returning, he planted them in the hills of Chikmagalur in present-day Karnataka. This area, now known as Baba Budangiri, became the birthplace of coffee cultivation in India and remains a significant coffee-growing region today. (Source: Coffee Board Of India)

How did coffee come to be associated with intellectual gatherings and revolution in 17th- and 18th-century Europe?

In cities like London and Paris, coffeehouses were called "penny universities" because for the price of a cup of coffee, people could engage in stimulating conversations about politics, philosophy, and science. These venues became hubs for Enlightenment thinking and even fomented political change — notably the French Revolution.

What role did the port of Mocha (in Yemen) play in the global spread of coffee?

Mocha was the world's first major coffee export hub in the 15th and 16th centuries. It became so central to the coffee trade that the term "mocha" is still used today to describe coffee — though ironically, little of today's "mocha" coffee contains beans from Yemen.

What is the origin of the word "coffee"?

: The word "coffee" comes from the Arabic word "qahwa", which originally referred to wine. It evolved into the Turkish "kahve", then to Dutch "koffie", and finally became "coffee" in English.

How did the Dutch contribute to the global coffee trade in the 17th century?

The Dutch were the first Europeans to cultivate coffee commercially outside of Arabia. They smuggled coffee plants from Yemen and began growing them in Java (Indonesia). This gave rise to Java as a coffee-growing region and helped break the Arab monopoly on coffee.
